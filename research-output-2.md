# **Comprehensive Source Discovery and Policy Analysis of Generative Artificial Intelligence Governance in Higher Education**

The integration of generative artificial intelligence into the global academic landscape has catalyzed a transition from traditional pedagogical frameworks toward dynamic, technology-mediated learning environments. As Large Language Models (LLMs) and other synthetic media generation tools become ubiquitous, universities are compelled to define the boundaries of "arrival technologies"—tools that enter the educational ecosystem through bottom-up adoption by students and faculty rather than through centralized procurement.1 This report provides an exhaustive investigation into the official policies, guidelines, and ethical frameworks established by leading academic institutions in India and the international community. The analysis focuses on the interplay between academic integrity, data privacy, and the preservation of human-centric critical inquiry.  
The institutional responses documented herein demonstrate a spectrum of governance strategies, ranging from prescriptive limitations to permissive, disclosure-based integration. A recurring theme across global domains is the shift toward "authentic assessments," which prioritize the cognitive process and critical interaction with technology over the final produced artifact.3 Furthermore, the emergence of specific quantitative thresholds for AI-assisted content in research indicates a move toward standardized metrics for academic originality in the digital age.5

## **IIT Delhi**

The Indian Institute of Technology Delhi represents a pioneering case in the Indian higher education sector, having formally codified guidelines for the responsible use of artificial intelligence following comprehensive internal evaluations. The institution's decision-making process was informed by significant empirical data; an internal survey revealed that approximately 80% of the student body and 77% of the faculty were already utilizing generative AI tools in their daily academic workflows.6 This high adoption rate necessitated a transition from implicit norms to explicit policy to prevent "unintended consequences" regarding academic work.6  
The core of the IIT Delhi framework is the principle of mandatory disclosure and transparency. Any academic or research work assisted by AI must be fully disclosed, specifically if the tools are used to create distinct elements such as data visualizations, tables, or significant textual sections.6 This disclosure is not merely an administrative requirement but is viewed as a mechanism to preserve academic integrity and ensure that the human author remains the primary agent of responsibility.6 Furthermore, the institution emphasizes "information security," advising the community to avoid inputting personally identifiable information (PII) or confidential institutional data into public-facing AI tools.6  
At the departmental level, particularly within the Computer Science and Engineering (CSE) and the Yardi School of AI (ScAI), these guidelines are operationalized through specific course policies. For instance, in advanced AI courses, collaboration policies are refined to include interactions with LLMs. The "Kyunki Saas Bhi Kabhi Bahu Thi Rule" adopted in some modules permits students to discuss assignments with peers or AI but prohibits taking away any written or electronic records from such meetings, forcing the student to reconstruct the solution independently after a "cooling-off" period of non-cognitive activity.7

## **IISc Bangalore**

The Indian Institute of Science (IISc) Bangalore has established a highly nuanced framework that differentiates between the use of AI for "editing" versus "content generation." According to the science faculty guidelines, tools like Grammarly, when used solely for style improvement and grammar correction, do not require disclosure.13 However, the use of AI to generate text, code, images, or tables must be transparently attributed in the Methods or Acknowledgements section.13 The institute emphasizes that while AI can provide reasoning and content generation, it is notoriously poor at judging "correctness," thereby placing the absolute burden of verification on the student or researcher.13  
A key institutional innovation at IISc is the "default allow with attribution" policy. In the absence of an explicit course policy, students are permitted to use AI tools provided they disclose the extent of that use.13 This proactive stance recognizes that banning AI is often "not prudent" and difficult to enforce due to the unreliability of automated AI detection software.14 Instead, the institute encourages faculty to rethink assessment design, suggesting a shift from unproctored take-home exams to in-class, supervised assessments or modified tasks where AI tools are unlikely to provide a significant advantage.14  
The IISc framework also addresses the ethical dimensions of research, particularly regarding "confidentiality" and "intellectual property." Researchers are cautioned that inputting sensitive content into AI tools is equivalent to "posting the content in public," as such data may be used to train the underlying models.14 Consequently, faculty are tasked with creating an "AI-responsible environment" within their research groups, requiring students to seek supervisor approval before integrating these tools into their scholarly work.14

## **IIT Madras**

## **Delhi University**

The University of Delhi (DU) has moved rapidly to quantify the permissible extent of AI usage in research. For undergraduate and postgraduate dissertations, the university has established a specific "20% limit" for AI-generated content.5 This quantitative threshold is verified through mandatory "AI detection certificates," which must accompany final report submissions.5 This regulation marks a significant shift from qualitative guidance to strict metrics-based policing of synthetic content.  
In addition to assessment limits, DU has integrated AI disclosure into its "Research Ethics and Compliance Standards" for academic journals. Journals approved by the university must require authors to disclose any AI-generated or AI-assisted content to maintain legitimacy.26 This policy is part of a broader "Prevention of Plagiarism" framework that aligns with the University Grants Commission (UGC) Level 0 plagiarism norms, requiring similarity indices to remain below 10%.26 The university also offers specialized "Certificate Programs" that explore AI policies and preparedness in fields like Public Health, emphasizing the "interactive and experiential" learning of AI models within ethical boundaries.28

## **MIT**

## **Stanford University**

Stanford University utilizes a nuanced "analogy" for AI governance: absent an explicit statement from an instructor, the use of generative AI is treated "analogously to receiving help from another person".37 This means that using AI to "substantially complete" an assignment or exam is a violation of the Honor Code.38 The university encourages a "default not allowed" approach, where students should assume AI use is prohibited unless the syllabus or assignment instructions state otherwise.37  
The Stanford Medical School has implemented a more rigorous policy due to the sensitive nature of patient data. Their policy strictly forbids the entry of "patient-identifying information" or "Protected Health Information" (PHI) into public AI platforms like ChatGPT or Gemini.40 Furthermore, any substantial contribution from AI in medical scholarly works must be disclosed with specific details, including the tool name, version, and the exact query used.40 The university also maintains an "Academic Integrity Working Group" (AIWG) that conducts proctoring pilots and develops "best practices" for in-person exams to mitigate the unauthorized use of digital aids.3

## **Harvard University**

Harvard University classifies data into four levels, with "Level 2 and above" (non-public research, student records, medical information) being strictly prohibited in public-facing AI tools.41 The university-wide guidelines mandate that all AI-generated content be reviewed before publication, as the human author is ultimately responsible for "hallucinations" or biased outputs.41  
The Harvard Graduate School of Education (HGSE) provides specific handbook language stating that AI should not be used to "do the cognitive work for you".43 HGSE permits AI for "brainstorming scenarios" or "seeking clarification" but defines the submission of AI-created text as a violation of academic integrity parallel to asking another person to complete your work.43 For researchers, Harvard Medical School (HMS) provides alignment with international standards like the Committee on Publication Ethics (COPE) and the World Association of Medical Editors (WAME), explicitly prohibiting AI from being listed as an author on scholarly papers.42

### **Comparative Assessment of Regional AI Governance Frameworks**

| Region | Policy Trend | Primary Enforcement Mechanism | Transparency Standard |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **India (IITs/DU)** | Metrics and Thresholds | AI Detection Certificates (DU) 5 | Full Disclosure of elements 6 |
| **North America (Harvard/MIT/Stanford/Toronto)** | Risk-Based Categorization | Data Levels (Harvard) 41 | Instructor Autonomy (Stanford) 38 |
| **Europe (ETH/Oxford/Cambridge/TUM)** | Human-in-the-Loop | Archiving Drafts (TUM) 74 | Formal Declaration (Oxford) 47 |
| **Asia-Pacific (NUS/Melbourne)** | Detailed Documentation | Prompt/Output Tables (NUS) 60 | Acknowledgment Tables (Melb) 69 |

The analysis confirms that while technical institutions (IITs, TUM, MIT) focus on "information security" and the "originality of code," comprehensive universities (Harvard, Oxford, DU) prioritize "research ethics" and "quantitative content limits." The consistent theme is that the "human author" remains the sole entity responsible for the academic record, regardless of the complexity of the AI assistant used.13 Institutions are increasingly moving toward "process evaluation" to ensure that the $P(w\_n | w\_1...w\_{n-1})$ probability of LLM outputs does not replace the critical inquiry required for scholarly advancement.64  
The evolution of these policies suggests a future where "AI Literacy" is as fundamental as "Library Research Skills," yet strictly governed to prevent the erosion of disciplinary expertise.40 The emergence of "AI-resistant" pedagogy further indicates that the traditional "take-home" essay may be nearing obsolescence in favor of supervised, multi-modal, or oral defense-based assessments.14
