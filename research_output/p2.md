# **Governance Frameworks for Generative Artificial Intelligence in Indian Higher Education: A Comprehensive Policy Analysis of Institutional Integrity and Digital Transformation**

The rapid ascendancy of Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) has precipitated a paradigm shift within the global academic landscape, necessitating a sophisticated recalibration of institutional policies to preserve digital integrity while fostering innovation. In India, the leading "Institutes of Eminence"—including the Indian Institutes of Technology (IITs), the Indian Institute of Science (IISc), and the Indian Institutes of Management (IIMs)—have transitioned from early skepticism toward a structured, "Responsible AI" framework. This report provides a granular examination of the policy architectures at IIT Bombay, IIT Delhi, IIT Madras, IISc Bangalore, and IIM Ahmedabad. These institutions are currently navigating the complexities of a "Sovereign AI" movement, where domestic foundational models like BharatGen are integrated into curricula that simultaneously demand rigorous transparency and the development of an "AI-Native" workforce.1  
The evolution of these policies is deeply rooted in the broader national context of the National Education Policy (NEP) 2020 and the recently unveiled India AI Governance Guidelines 2025\. These national directives emphasize "Innovation over Restraint" and "People First" principles, which are mirrored in the institutional mandates of the universities under review.1 As these premier institutions adapt, they are moving beyond simple anti-plagiarism rules to comprehensive "Generative AI Use Policies" that govern everything from classroom pedagogy and coding assistance to doctoral research and institutional data privacy.5

## **Indian Institute of Technology (IIT) Bombay, India**

IIT Bombay has adopted a strategic stance that positions Generative AI as a "foundational business imperative" and a critical driver of "Learning-Centric Education." The institution’s governance is not confined to a single prohibitive document but is instead distributed across its "Strategy Plan 2026–2030," specialized school-level guidelines (such as those from the Desai Sethi School of Entrepreneurship), and high-level academic-industrial partnerships.3

### **Institutional Philosophy and Digital Integrity**

The institution recognizes that GenAI marks "one of the most significant technological shifts of our time," reshaping the creation of ideas and the organization of knowledge.9 Consequently, the policy focuses on building "capability over curiosity," emphasizing that students must understand and apply AI in a responsible and effective manner.9 The "Strategy Plan 2026–2030" explicitly identifies AI and Machine Learning as priority research domains, aiming to elevate the Institute's global leadership in frontier research while ensuring the "wellness of students" through modernized management and ethical oversight.3  
A significant component of the IIT Bombay framework is the "BharatGen" initiative. As the world’s first government-funded generative AI project, it aims to create multilingual foundational models that ensure "inclusive and efficient AI across 22+ Indian languages".3 This sovereign push implies an institutional preference for indigenous models that are "inclusive and trusted," reflecting a policy move toward "technological sovereignty".3

## **Indian Institute of Technology (IIT) Delhi, India**

IIT Delhi has taken a pioneering role by formally establishing a "Committee on the use of Generative AI" as early as April 2024\.17 The institution's guidelines are designed to sensitize the campus community to the risks of over-reliance on technology, which "may hinder essential learning outcomes such as critical thinking skills".17

### **Survey-Driven Policy Development**

The IIT Delhi policy is uniquely grounded in empirical data from its own campus. A survey conducted by the committee involved 427 students and 88 faculty members, revealing that 80% of students were already regular users of GenAI tools.5 Interestingly, while 52% of students felt these tools did not raise professional ethical concerns, faculty members expressed significant anxiety regarding "risks to intellectual integrity" and the potential for "intellectual dishonesty".17  
The Director of IIT Delhi, Prof. Rangan Banerjee, emphasized that the widespread adoption of these tools prompts a reconsideration of "classroom teaching, design assignments and exams".18 The resulting policy mandate is clear: "full disclosure of any such work" is required to uphold academic integrity.18

## **Indian Institute of Technology (IIT) Madras, India**

IIT Madras has positioned itself at the center of the national AI policy dialogue. The institution’s philosophy is built upon "Sovereign AI" and the "Responsible AI (RAI)" framework, prioritizing fairness, accountability, and safety.22 The "AI Policy" at IIT Madras is characterized by "continuous policy tweaks" to remain relevant amid geopolitical shifts.24

### **Governance through Specialized Centers**

The Wadhwani School of Data Science and AI at IIT Madras leads the "Responsible AI Tour," an executive certificate program that introduces participants to algorithmic issues and principles of RAI, such as transparency and human-centered values.22 This educational focus serves as the institution’s primary mechanism for policy dissemination. Furthermore, the "AI for Teachers" certification course equipped K12 educators with essential skills for integrating AI responsibly into pedagogy.25  
Prof. Balaraman Ravindran’s chairmanship of the MeitY expert committee underscores the institute's commitment to the "Do No Harm" guiding principle, focusing on "risk mitigation within a flexible, adaptive system".26

## **Indian Institute of Science (IISc) Bangalore, India**

IISc Bangalore, India’s foremost research university, established the "IISc Committee on AI Tools for Research and Education" (I-CARE) in August 2023\.6 The committee recognized that "banning access to these tools is not prudent" given their ready availability and potential for positive implications for education and research.6

### **The Four-Category Policy Framework**

IISc has adopted a sophisticated "Default Policy" mechanism. Course policies at the institute are categorized into four groups: **Prohibited, Allowed with Attribution, Encouraged,** or **Mandatory**.7 Crucially, if a course lacks an explicit policy, the default institutional policy is "allowed with attribution".7  
The institution distinguishes between different levels of AI involvement. For "open AI tools," disclosure is mandatory if new content (text, images, tables, code) is generated. However, disclosure is waived for "typing assistants" like Grammarly that simply edit and improve existing text.7

## **Synthesis of National and Institutional AI Policies**

The institutional policies at India’s premier academic centers are inextricably linked to the "National AI for All" strategy. This national roadmap, guided by the seven "Sutras of Trust," provides a technology-agnostic framework applicable across all sectors, including higher education.1

### **The Role of "Sovereign AI" in Academic Integrity**

A second-order insight derived from the institutional data is the prioritization of "Sovereign AI." By developing domestic Large Language Models like "BharatGen" (IIT Bombay) and "AIKosh" (MeitY), the Indian academic ecosystem is attempting to mitigate the risks of data mapped by foreign firms.2 This is not merely a technical goal but a policy imperative to "protect official systems from foreign AI services".26

### **Digital Integrity and the "Human-in-the-Loop"**

The recurring "Human-in-the-Loop" requirement across IIT Bombay, IIT Delhi, and IIM Ahmedabad serves as the fundamental mechanism for digital integrity. This policy acknowledges that while AI can "accelerate breakthroughs," it cannot substitute "human judgement".3 The "Delegate, but Validate" maxim ensures that the accountability for intellectual property and factual accuracy remains with the human scholar.16

## **Future Outlook and Policy Implications**

The trajectory of GenAI policy in Indian higher education suggests an "inflexion year" in 2025/2026, where paradigm shifts led by AI become organically embedded from the foundational stages of education.1 The transition from "experimentation to strategic integration" requires institutions to move beyond isolated pilots to system-wide transformations.46

### **Transition to Competency-Based Assessment**

The "inevitable shift in assessment methods" is perhaps the most profound impact of GenAI on digital integrity. Institutions are moving away from rote memorization toward assessments that engage students in "critical thinking" and "practical application of knowledge".47 This transition ensures that the legitimacy and trustworthiness of academic endeavors are sustained in the digital era.47

### **Addressing the AI Literacy Gap**

To prevent a "deterioration in academic integrity," institutions must prioritize "AI literacy across disciplines".44 This includes mandatory training for both students and faculty on "ethical study practices" and the "responsible use of technology".21 The goal is to empower a future generation that is not just knowledgeable about AI but is "AI-ready" for a labor market where 40% of core skills are projected to change by 2030\.36  
In conclusion, the policy landscape at India’s top institutions is characterized by a sophisticated balance between embracing the "century-defining opportunities" of AI and safeguarding the core values of "fairness, honesty, trust, respect, and responsibility".32 By integrating sovereign AI models, mandatory disclosure frameworks, and a "Human-in-the-Loop" validation process, these universities are successfully charting a course for the responsible use of Generative AI in higher education.
