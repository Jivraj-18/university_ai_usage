# **Navigating the Algorithmic Frontier: A Comprehensive Analysis of Generative AI Policies and Academic Integrity Frameworks in Premier Indian Universities**

The integration of Generative Artificial Intelligence into the pedagogical and research structures of Indian higher education represents one of the most significant shifts in academic policy since the digital revolution of the late twentieth century. As institutions of higher learning grapple with the dualities of innovation and integrity, a complex landscape of guidelines, prohibitions, and conditional permissions has emerged. This report examines the institutional frameworks of five leading Indian universities: Delhi University, Jadavpur University, BITS Pilani, Manipal Institute of Technology, and Ashoka University. By analyzing official policy documents, provost statements, and departmental guidelines, the following discourse identifies the mechanisms through which these institutions maintain the rigors of scholarly inquiry while acknowledging the transformative potential of large language models and other algorithmic tools.

## **Delhi University: Centralized Directives and Specialized Legal Integrity**

The University of Delhi operates as a multifaceted ecosystem where centralized postgraduate frameworks intersect with specialized departmental policies. While the broader university has issued notifications regarding the inclusion of AI ethics in curricula, the most granular and authoritative policy documents reside within its constituent legal and technological units. The National Law University Delhi (NLU Delhi), an affiliated but autonomous legal institution, has promulgated a particularly rigorous "AI Policy" that serves as a benchmark for disclosure-based academic integrity.

The framework established by NLU Delhi emphasizes "intellectual accountability" and "transparency" as the primary defenses against algorithmic misuse.1 By classifying GenAI as a "support function" rather than a primary generator of content, the policy reinforces the traditional academic value that intellectual synthesis must remain a human endeavor. The inclusion of a mandatory "AI Use Statement" ensures that the boundary between machine assistance and human authorship is clearly demarcated.1 This is particularly critical in legal education, where the phenomenon of "hallucinated authorities"—fictitious cases or laws generated by AI—presents a direct threat to the integrity of legal scholarship and practice.1 The policy’s enforcement mechanism is robust, specifying that "non-disclosure shall constitute academic misconduct," thereby aligning AI misuse with existing disciplinary frameworks for plagiarism and data falsification.1

While a single, university-wide "Master Policy" is not publicly cited as a standalone document, the institution's commitment is evident through its journal editorial policies, smart-library initiatives, and department-specific workshops. These guidelines focus on the practical utility of AI in improving research efficiency while maintaining strict barriers against authorship credit for non-human entities.

## **BITS Pilani: Redefining Authorship and Assessment Methodology**

The Birla Institute of Technology and Science (BITS) Pilani has emerged as a vocal leader in the debate over GenAI integrity. Under the guidance of Vice-Chancellor Ramgopal Rao, the institution has formally recognized the "blurring line between assistance and authorship".9 BITS Pilani's approach is notably pedagogical, choosing to sensitize students to the "science behind the AI operation" rather than imposing a blanket prohibition that may prove logistically impossible to enforce.9

The BITS Pilani model represents a pivot from "detection" to "deterrence through education." By integrating AI into the first-year curriculum, the institution acknowledges that GenAI is a permanent fixture of the professional landscape.9 However, the definition of "malpractice" remains strictly tied to the act of presenting AI output without "understanding or acknowledgement".9 A critical policy shift identified at BITS is the elevation of the *viva voce* (oral examination) as the "best way to ascertain" student effort, effectively signaling that the written product alone is no longer a sufficient proxy for learning in the age of ChatGPT.9 Furthermore, the Institute's Data Privacy Policy ensures that any "Processing" of data—defined to include automated structuring and alignment—is strictly governed by the Digital Personal Data Protection Act of 2023\.10

## **Manipal Academy of Higher Education (MAHE): Strategic Partnerships and Human-Centered Integration**

Manipal Academy of Higher Education, particularly through the Manipal Institute of Technology (MIT), has adopted one of the most proactive stances toward Generative AI in the Indian subcontinent. ## **Ashoka University: Liberal Arts and the "Scaffolding" Framework**

Ashoka University has developed one of the most pedagogically sophisticated frameworks for GenAI, situating the technology within its "interdisciplinary liberal arts approach".17 The university’s Centre for Writing and Communication (CWC) has released findings suggesting that AI should be used as a "supplementary aid" to "scaffold the thinking process" while preserving "human agency".18 Ashoka is unique in its development of "AI-Resistant Pedagogical Frameworks" designed to ensure that core argumentation skills are not eroded by algorithmic assistance.19

A critical third-order insight from the Ashoka documentation is the explicit warning against "techno-nationalism" and "biases" inherent in GenAI software, leading to ongoing projects aimed at "decolonising generative AI".24 Furthermore, the university identifies a significant ethical conflict in using AI for "peer review," classifying it as a "Red" status due to the breach of confidentiality inherent in uploading others' work to a proprietary model.21

## **Comparative Thematic Analysis of Institutional Responses**

A synthesis of the guidelines across these five institutions reveals a core set of emerging themes that define the "Indian Model" of AI academic policy. Unlike the purely prohibitive models often seen in early 2023, these institutions have moved toward a "Disclosure-Centric" and "Process-Oriented" framework.

### **The "Yellow" Consensus: Conditional Permission as the New Standard**

The vast majority of academic tasks—from coding to literature reviews—have shifted into the "Yellow" category. This transition is not an endorsement of unrestricted use, but a recognition that "blanket bans" are unenforceable. Instead, institutions like NLU Delhi and Ashoka focus on "transparency" and "human agency".1 The table below compares the threshold for "Yellow" status across the universities.

| Institution | Threshold for Conditional Use | Key Verification Mechanism |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Delhi University / NLUD** | Mandatory Disclosure Statement | Human verification of citations 1 |
| **Jadavpur University** | Linguistic/Visualization support only | Non-authorship certification 6 |
| **BITS Pilani** | Understanding of "Science behind AI" | Viva Voce / Oral Defense 9 |
| **MAHE / MIT** | Treats AI as a "Starting Point" | Comparison of AI vs. Manual work 13 |
| **Ashoka University** | Scaffolding/Thinking assistance | Reflective input layer (SocraticAI) 23 |

### **The "Red" Barriers: Absolute Prohibitions and Intellectual Redlines**

Despite the move toward integration, three distinct "Red" areas remain across all sampled institutions:

1. **Authorship Credit:** AI can never be an author. This is a non-negotiable standard driven by the lack of "intellectual responsibility" and "accountability".1  
2. **Confidential Data Uploads:** Uploading unpublished research, personal student data (under FERPA/DPDPA), or sensitive medical information is strictly forbidden.10  
3. **Unacknowledged Reproduction:** The reproduction of AI output as one's own work is categorized as "academic malpractice" or "egregious violation".1

## **Philosophical and Regulatory Implications**

The institutional policies examined here do not exist in isolation; they are deeply influenced by the evolving national regulatory environment. The "Digital Personal Data Protection Act (2023)" is frequently cited as the legal backbone for data privacy on campuses.10 Furthermore, the tension between "copyright" and "training" is recognized at the policy level, with institutions warning that AI outputs may "derive unlawfully from copyrighted material," thus shifting the legal burden to the student.1

### **The Evolution of the "Searchable Fact"**

One of the most profound ripple effects of GenAI on these policies is the changing nature of the "fact." Both BITS Pilani and NLU Delhi warn against "untraceable references" and "hallucinated authorities".1 This has led to a re-institutionalization of "manual verification" as a core academic skill. In the policy framework of 2025-2026, the ability to *verify* information is prioritized as highly as the ability to *find* it.4

### **Conclusion**

The research indicates that Delhi University, Jadavpur, BITS Pilani, MAHE, and Ashoka have successfully navigated the initial disruption of Generative AI by embedding it within existing frameworks of academic integrity. The common denominator across these institutions is the "Human-in-the-Loop" principle. Whether through mandatory viva voce at BITS Pilani, the AI Use Statement at NLU Delhi, or the Socratic prompts at Ashoka, the burden of intellectual labor remains firmly on the human student. The future outlook suggests a continued integration of AI into the "formative" stages of learning—brainstorming, outlining, and linguistic polishing—while maintaining a "Red" status for AI in the "summative" creation of core arguments and knowledge production. By moving from "detection" to "pedagogical scaffolding," these universities are ensuring that the value of the degree remains tied to human critical competence in an increasingly algorithmic world.
